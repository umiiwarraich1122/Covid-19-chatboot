Mechanism of Action: Designed to induce neutralizing antibodies and cellular immune responses (T-cell activation) against the SARS-CoV-2 spike protein. mRNA vaccines (e.g., Pfizer-BioNTech, Moderna) deliver lipid nanoparticle-encapsulated mRNA instructing host cells to transiently express the spike protein. Protein subunit vaccines (e.g., Novavax) deliver recombinant spike protein directly alongside an adjuvant to enhance the immunogenic response.

Indications & Efficacy: Indicated for active immunization to prevent COVID-19, with a primary clinical benefit of significantly reducing the risk of severe disease, hospitalization, and death. While vaccine-induced sterilizing immunity against infection wanes over time and with the emergence of novel viral variants, cellular immunity remains highly protective against severe outcomes. Formulations are periodically updated (monovalent strain updates) to antigenically match dominant circulating lineages.

Safety & Adverse Events: Generally safe and well-tolerated. Common adverse effects are mild-to-moderate and self-limiting, including local injection site reactions (pain, erythema, swelling) and systemic responses (fatigue, headache, myalgia, low-grade fever). Rare but serious adverse events include myocarditis and pericarditis, primarily observed in young males within a week of receiving mRNA doses, and historically, thrombosis with thrombocytopenia syndrome (TTS) associated with adenoviral vector platforms.

Administration & Clinical Considerations: Administered intramuscularly, typically into the deltoid muscle. Dosing schedules, including primary series and periodic updated booster doses, are stratified by age, immunocompromise status, and previous vaccination history. Absolute contraindications are limited to a documented history of severe allergic reaction (anaphylaxis) to a previous dose or to any component of the vaccine (e.g., polyethylene glycol).
