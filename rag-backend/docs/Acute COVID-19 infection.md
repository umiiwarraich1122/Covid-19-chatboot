Acute COVID-19 Infection
Pathophysiology: Infection by the SARS-CoV-2 virus, which binds to host angiotensin-converting enzyme 2 (ACE2) receptors in the respiratory tract. This facilitates viral entry, replication, and a subsequent inflammatory response. In severe cases, this triggers an exaggerated immune response (cytokine storm), leading to alveolar damage, microvascular thrombosis, and systemic endothelial dysfunction.

Clinical features: Highly variable, ranging from asymptomatic to severe respiratory failure. Common symptoms include fever, cough, dyspnea, fatigue, myalgias, loss of taste or smell (anosmia/ageusia), sore throat, congestion, and gastrointestinal symptoms (nausea, diarrhea). Severe cases progress to acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS) and multi-organ failure.

Diagnosis: Confirmed by detection of SARS-CoV-2 viral RNA via real-time reverse transcription-polymerase chain reaction (RT-PCR) or viral antigen detection using rapid diagnostic tests (RDTs) on nasopharyngeal or nasal swabs. Supporting investigations in severe cases include chest imaging (showing bilateral ground-glass opacities) and elevated inflammatory markers (CRP, ferritin, D-dimer).

Management: For mild-to-moderate disease in outpatients at high risk of progression, treatment includes antiviral therapy (e.g., ritonavir-boosted nirmatrelvir [Paxlovid] or remdesivir) initiated early in the disease course, alongside supportive care (antipyretics, hydration). For hospitalized patients requiring supplemental oxygen, management includes systemic corticosteroids (e.g., dexamethasone), intravenous remdesivir, and immunomodulatory therapy (e.g., baricitinib or tocilizumab) as indicated. Prophylactic anticoagulation is routinely administered to hospitalized patients to prevent venous thromboembolism unless contraindicated.
