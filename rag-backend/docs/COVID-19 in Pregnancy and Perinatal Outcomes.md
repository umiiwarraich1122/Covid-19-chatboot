Pathophysiology: Pregnancy induces physiological alterations, including a shift toward a Th2-dominant immunological environment, elevated diaphragm levels reducing functional residual capacity, and increased oxygen consumption. These changes make pregnant individuals highly vulnerable to severe respiratory pathogens. SARS-CoV-2 can infect the placenta by binding to ACE2 receptors expressed in syncytiotrophoblasts. Although placental pathology (such as histiocytic intervillositis and fibrin deposition) can occur, true transplacental vertical transmission to the fetus is rare (estimated at <2–3% of cases).

Clinical features: While a majority of pregnant individuals with SARS-CoV-2 infection remain asymptomatic, those who do develop symptoms typically present with fever, cough, dyspnea, and myalgias. Pregnant individuals are at a significantly higher risk for severe illness, ICU admission, and mechanical ventilation compared to age-matched non-pregnant individuals. Severe maternal infection is strongly associated with adverse perinatal outcomes, including an increased risk of preeclampsia, gestational diabetes, iatrogenic preterm birth, stillbirth, and neonatal ICU admission.

Diagnosis: Confirmed via standard RT-PCR or rapid antigen testing of nasopharyngeal swabs. When evaluating a symptomatic pregnant patient, key diagnostic tools include pulse oximetry to monitor maternal oxygenation and fetal heart rate monitoring (if at a viable gestational age). For patients with severe respiratory distress, chest imaging (using a shield to protect the fetus) or bedside lung ultrasound is utilized to assess for bilateral pneumonia. Detailed fetal anatomical ultrasounds are recommended for those infected in the first trimester, alongside third-trimester growth scans to monitor for fetal growth restriction.
