Pathophysiology: Post-COVID conditions (also known as Long COVID) is a multisystem syndrome persisting after acute SARS-CoV-2 infection. Proposed mechanisms include persistent viral reservoirs (viral persistence), autoantibody production triggering autoimmune pathways, chronic low-grade tissue inflammation, microvascular endothelial injury with microclots, and dysregulation of the autonomic nervous system (dysautonomia).

Clinical features: Characterized by symptoms persisting, worsening, or developing at least three months after the initial infection, lasting for at least two months, and which cannot be explained by an alternative diagnosis. The most common presentations include profound fatigue, post-exertional malaise (PEM), cognitive impairment ("brain fog"), dyspnea, palpitations, chest pain, joint pain, sleep disturbances, and orthostatic intolerance (such as postural orthostatic tachycardia syndrome, or POTS).

Diagnosis: Primarily a clinical diagnosis of exclusion. Thorough history and physical examination are essential to rule out other underlying cardiac, pulmonary, or neurological etiologies. Basic diagnostic workup typically includes a complete blood count, metabolic panel, thyroid function tests, inflammatory markers (CRP, ESR), cardiac enzymes, and an electrocardiogram (ECG). Chest imaging or pulmonary function tests may be indicated for persistent respiratory symptoms.

Management: Treatment is highly individualized, multidisciplinary, and focused on symptom management and functional rehabilitation. Key strategies include pacing and energy conservation to manage post-exertional malaise (avoiding "push-and-crash" cycles), targeted pharmacotherapy for specific symptoms (e.g., beta-blockers or fludrocortisone for POTS, low-dose naltrexone for neuroinflammation), cognitive pacing, and psychological support. Gradual, symptom-titrated physical activity should only be introduced cautiously once PEM is stabilized.
