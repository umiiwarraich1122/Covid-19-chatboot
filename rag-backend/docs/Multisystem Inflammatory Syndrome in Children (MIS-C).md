Pathophysiology: A rare but severe post-infectious hyperinflammatory condition occurring typically 2 to 6 weeks after acute SARS-CoV-2 infection in children and adolescents. The mechanism involves an aberrant, delayed immune response characterized by superantigen-like T-cell activation, massive cytokine release (cytokine storm), autoantibody production, and widespread endothelial dysfunction leading to systemic vasculitis.Clinical features: Characterized by persistent fever (typically $\ge 38.0^\circ\text{C}$ lasting $\ge 3$ days) and multi-organ involvement. Common presentations include prominent gastrointestinal symptoms (severe abdominal pain, vomiting, diarrhea, often mimicking appendicitis), mucocutaneous findings (non-purulent conjunctivitis, polymorphic rash, strawberry tongue, hand/foot edema), cardiovascular dysfunction (hypotension, shock, myocarditis), and neurological symptoms (headache, lethargy).Diagnosis: Based on clinical criteria alongside laboratory evidence of systemic inflammation. Key findings include markedly elevated inflammatory markers (CRP, ESR, procalcitonin, ferritin) and cardiac biomarkers (NT-proBNP, troponin). Diagnostic criteria require evidence of recent SARS-CoV-2 infection (positive RT-PCR, antigen, or serology), involvement of two or more organ systems, fever, and the exclusion of alternative infectious diagnoses (such as bacterial sepsis). An echocardiogram is mandatory to evaluate for coronary artery aneurysms and myocardial dysfunction.Management: Requires hospitalization, often in a pediatric intensive care unit (PICU) for hemodynamic monitoring. First-line medical therapy consists of intravenous immunoglobulin (IVIG) combined with systemic corticosteroids (e.g., methylprednisolone). Low-dose aspirin is initiated for its antiplatelet effects to prevent coronary artery thrombosis, unless contraindicated by active bleeding. In refractory cases, biologic agents targeting specific cytokines, such as anakinra (IL-1 inhibitor) or infliximab (TNF-alpha inhibitor), are utilized.
